Span XFRA
A distributed AI data center
in the backyard.

Span just announced a plan to put a 16-GPU AI inference pod — HVAC-sized, sitting on a concrete pad in the side yard — at 80,000 American homes. The hardware is real. The economics are not yet proven.

This deck is the strict reading of what the evidence actually supports — across the technical claim, the unit economics, the strategic position, the risk surface, and the opportunities the analysis opens up. We build the argument one slide at a time. By the end, we get to the three questions that decide the whole bet.

Northwest Ohio could be inside this footprint. Cheap PJM/AEP power, dense single-family housing across Toledo, Findlay, Lima and Bowling Green, expanding middle-mile fiber, and a workforce already absorbing data-center jobs from the New Albany corridor — the region has the inputs an 80,000-home distributed buildout actually needs. The question for NW Ohio isn't whether the wave arrives; it's whether the region is on the host list or the bypass list.

What if we used this technology in downtown Toledo?

Technical viability

Start with the thing itself.

A quarter-million dollars of conventional AI hardware in an outdoor HVAC-sized box. Nothing exotic — and that's the point.

The pod is a conventional enterprise AI inference server in an industrialized outdoor box. Roughly the size of an HVAC condenser. Every component ships today. The expense is real — and it's that expense that forces the economic question on the next slides.

Business viability

The model reduces to four numbers per node.

Three numbers are knowable today. The fourth — R, the revenue Span gets per node — is the one they haven't announced. Everything else cascades from it.

Span's whole pitch reduces to one piece of arithmetic. Three of the four numbers are observable today. The fourth — what a hyperscaler will pay — is the one Span needs to land and hasn't yet announced.

Strategic positioning

The moat is a combination, not any single asset.

No single asset is a moat. The combination of installed base + builder channel + power-control IP holds for 3–4 years. That's the window.

Assume the economics clear. The next question is defensibility. None of Span's assets is individually a moat. The combination — five years of installed panels, a top-three homebuilder channel, and the regulatory work to get power-control authority on a residential panel — is reasonably hard to copy in under three or four years.

Pulte partnership
Pulte can deploy with others; logo-strong but contractually unknown.
Smart panel installed base
5+ years of installs; real switching cost for builders standardized on Span.
Power-control IP & certification
Long process to UL-list and PUC-clear a panel with this authority.
Orchestration software
Pure software; replicable by any well-funded competitor.
Hardware design
Integration of commodity parts; no proprietary silicon.
Tariff & regulatory relationships
Slow to build, slow to copy.
First-mover narrative
Decays as competitors enter.

Three to four years is the structural window. After that, defensibility depends on whether anchor contracts and tariff treatments lock in distribution.

Adjacencies · part 1 of 2

Things Span could ship in 2027.

Four moves that reuse the existing investment — installed panel base, hardware envelope, orchestration software. No new business needed; just orchestration and packaging changes.

The first batch of adjacencies is the cheapest. The same platform Span is already running for XFRA can do four other things — selling demand response to utilities, packing more racks into a host site, capturing waste heat for hot water, and bundling its own risk into an insurance product.

Nearest term
Utility grid-services revenue
Sell demand-response capacity to utilities alongside compute resale. The battery + panel + orchestration stack is already there. No incremental capex.
Nearest term
Multi-rack site upgrade path
2–4 racks per non-residential host unlocks tightly-coupled workloads — training, larger inference. Same enclosure design; bigger pad.
Medium-term
Heat reuse for residential hot water
12 kW IT load rejected as low-grade heat. In cold climates, 30–50% winter heating offset for hosts. Turns "free electricity" into "free electricity and free hot water."
Margin lever
Insurance-as-a-product
Glycol, cooling, and fire risk at residential sites creates a real bundled insurance product. 10–20% margin lift at fleet scale; also de-risks the host conversation.

All four reuse what's already built. Next: shifts that reach entirely different customers.

Four shifts that reach different buyers, different geographies, different businesses — all reusing Span's existing engineering, panel IP, and hardware envelope.

The second batch of adjacencies asks Span to reach beyond the residential-channel + hyperscaler-buyer model. Schools and civic buildings are a different buyer. The Northeast is a different market. The DOD is a different sales cycle. Telcos and retailers want the hardware without the service. All four reuse the same engineering work.

Overall assessment

Strip everything else away.

Three questions decide it. None is answerable today. All three are answerable in the next 18 months.

The bet comes down to three questions. None of them is answerable from public information today. All three are answerable within the next 18 months. Pilot data, the first PUC ruling, and the first hyperscaler contract will resolve the whole thing.

01
Does the orchestrator deliver hyperscaler-grade SLA across a residential fleet?
02
Does at least one PUC, in at least one high-value market, approve a tariff that lets Span pay below retail for marginal kWh?
03
Does at least one hyperscaler sign a multi-year anchor contract at edge-premium pricing?

A "yes" on all three makes XFRA a category-defining business with a 3–4 year window. A "no" on any one confines it to a profitable-but-bounded Southwest business — a real outcome, not the gigawatt ambition.

In closing

The bet is reasonable. The binary risks are public.

XFRA is a credible, well-staged attempt to convert a real structural inefficiency — residential grid underutilization — into a real economic asset: saleable AI inference capacity.

The hardware works, the integration is solvable, the pilot is sized to test the right thing. The thesis is not crazy.

But the binary risks — orchestrator SLA, PUC tariff outcomes, anchor-tenant pricing — are concentrated in places Span has not yet pressure-tested in public. The next 18 months resolve most of the load-bearing uncertainty. Track the three questions on slide 12; everything else is detail.

Northwest Ohio · concept

A regional opportunity worth taking seriously.

Northwest Ohio has the manufacturing base, the skilled workforce, and (with heat reuse) the climate profile to turn an XFRA-type product into a regional industrial story — not just a deployment site.

Everything up to this point has been strict analysis. The next four slides shift mode — they ask what an XFRA-style product could mean for Toledo and Northwest Ohio if we wanted to participate, not just observe. Speculative, but concrete enough to act on.

Ahead: three regional advantages — we can build it, install it, and the use case fits us better — and then what a Toledo pilot might look like.

Northwest Ohio · concept · build (1 of 3)

First — we can build it.

The XFRA pod isn't exotic — it's HVAC-form-factor metalwork with liquid cooling and insulation. Every layer of that supply chain lives within 30 minutes of Toledo. Span ships pods from California; we could fabricate them here.

Most analyses of XFRA treat the deployment region as a passive host. Northwest Ohio's industrial base would change that relationship. The same supply chain that built the auto industry and the glass industry can build outdoor compute enclosures.

Manufacturing
Outdoor enclosure supply chain
The pod is steel-and-aluminum housing with insulation, liquid-cooling loops, and weatherproofing. Every layer has a regional supplier: Owens Corning (Toledo HQ — insulation, composites, glass-fiber reinforcement); Dana Incorporated (Maumee — thermal management, liquid-cooling expertise from decades of automotive driveline work); regional metal fabrication from the same shops that supply Stellantis Toledo Assembly; and NSG/Pilkington for any glass elements.
Span ships pods from California. We could fabricate them here at lower cost and shorter lead time.
Solar / PV adjacency
First Solar at Perrysburg
North America's largest thin-film PV manufacturing facility sits 15 minutes south of Toledo. Recently expanded through a multi-billion-dollar investment, First Solar's Series 7 production keeps PV modules in the regional supply chain — not the global one. When XFRA pairs with behind-the-meter solar (the slide-10 optional add-on), the modules don't need to cross an ocean to reach a Toledo install.
Solar pairing becomes a regional supply story, not a logistics problem.

Building the pod is step one. Next: installing and servicing it.

Northwest Ohio · concept · install (2 of 3)

Second — we can install and service it.

Building the pod is one thing. Getting it into 50 homes and keeping it running for 5 years is another. Toledo's electrical trades and homebuilder presence make both faster than fly-in installs from California.

A pilot is a logistics exercise as much as an engineering one. The trades, the apprenticeships, and the homebuilder channel all exist here — no fly-in crew required.

Workforce
Skilled trades pipeline
Installing an XFRA pod needs three trades: electrical (200A service tie-in, panel work), HVAC (cooling loop, heat-pump rejection), and plumbing for the heat-reuse adjacency. IBEW Local 8 has thousands of electricians in the Toledo area. Every neighborhood already has an HVAC tech who can work a heat pump. Owens Community College runs registered apprenticeships in all three trades. University of Toledo engineering and BGSU College of Engineering and Innovation produce the technical talent for orchestration and supervisory roles.
No fly-in California crew needed. The trades are already here.
Builder channel
Active homebuilder presence
Span's pilot model depends on builder pre-installation — the panel, conduit, and pad get installed before move-in. Toledo metro has active operations from Pulte, NVR/Ryan, Drees, and regional builders. They're building in Sylvania, Maumee, Perrysburg, Monclova, Oregon, and Holland. Volume is much smaller than Phoenix or Las Vegas — maybe 200–500 new builds per major builder per year — but the relationship-and-process side of the channel exists and works.
Lower volume means slower ramp, but also lower ops risk and faster iteration cycles.

Northwest Ohio · concept · fit (3 of 3)

Third — the use case fits us better.

The Sun Belt pilot has cheap power but wastes the rejected heat. We have a colder climate AND a latency hub within reach of ~30 million people. Two structural advantages the Sun Belt can't claim.

The Sun Belt argument for XFRA is cheap power and new construction. The Northwest Ohio argument is different — and arguably stronger: heat that gets used instead of dumped, plus a population center that benefits from edge-inference latency.

Climate
Cold winters turn rejected heat into a feature
A 12 kW IT load's rejected heat goes to waste in Phoenix — the cooling system just dumps it. In Toledo, that same heat becomes a real co-benefit. Captured into a pre-heat loop for the host's domestic water, it offsets natural-gas heating cost estimated $500–1,000/yr per node depending on home size and gas rates. The host signs up for the heat, not just the bill credit. The slide-10 adjacency that's optional for Span becomes our primary value prop.
What's a thermal liability in Phoenix is a thermal asset in Toledo.
Latency
We're 30 ms from ~30 million people
Toledo sits at a Midwest crossroads — within ~30 ms RTT of Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland, Columbus, and Indianapolis. That's roughly 30 million people in the consumer-AI latency window for any inference workload that benefits from being close to end users — AR/VR, agentic chat, real-time recommendation, autonomous-vehicle inference. Span's Southwest pilot proximity argument works for the Sun Belt; Toledo's argument works for the Great Lakes corridor.
Edge inference for the Midwest doesn't need to live in Phoenix.

Northwest Ohio · concept · what it looks like

A Toledo pilot would look different — and that's the point.

50-home pilot in Sylvania, Maumee, or West Toledo. Enclosures built locally. Heat reuse as the primary host value. Different inputs, different math, different story — one that includes a regional manufacturing angle Span's Southwest pilot can't tell.

Three differences from Span's Southwest pilot — and each one tilts in Toledo's favor.

Difference 1
Local manufacture instead of CA ship
Outdoor enclosures fabricated from regional Owens Corning / Dana / metalwork supply chain. Cuts shipping cost, shortens lead times, creates a regional jobs story that local utilities and PUCs will reward in tariff conversations.
Difference 2
Heat reuse as primary value
A 12 kW IT load rejects heat year-round. In Toledo winters, capturing it pre-warms the host's domestic water and offsets natural-gas heating cost — estimated $500–1,000/yr per node depending on home size. Hosts sign up for the heat, not just the bill credit.
Difference 3
Workforce-led install
Builder partner + IBEW Local 8 electricians + HVAC trades + AEP Ohio coordination. Local trades install faster than Span's CA crew can fly out — and the work stays in the local economy, which makes the political conversation easier.
Who's at the table
Anchor partners to validate
First Solar (PV pairing). Pulte / NVR / Drees (builder channel). AEP Ohio or Toledo Edison (tariff conversation). University of Toledo PVIC (technical validation). BGSU College of Engineering and Innovation (engineering capacity + research partner). Owens Community College (workforce pipeline). Regional Growth Partnership and TMACOG (economic-development cover).

This is concept, not commitment. Next step: a six-meeting roadshow — Pulte regional, AEP Ohio, First Solar BD, UToledo PVIC, BGSU College of Engineering and Innovation, and RGP — to see who'd put their name on a pilot.
